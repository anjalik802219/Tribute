Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, in full Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, (born October 15, 1931, Rameswaram, India - died July 27, 2015, Shillong), Indian scientist and politician who played a leading role in the development of India's missile and nuclear weapons programs. He was president of India from 2002 to 2007.He is a man of vision, who is always full of ideas aimed at the development of the country.

Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam Father, Jainulabdeen was a boat owner and imam of a local mosque and his mother Ashiamma was a housewife. Kalam was the youngest of four brothers and one sister in his family.

He completed its education at the Schwartz Higher Secondary School, Ramanathapuram. He graduated in physics 1954 fromSaint Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli. He moved to Madras in 1955 to study aerospace engineering in Madras Institute of Technology.

Notable Achievements

  • Achievement 1: Career Achievements of Dr. Kalam : In the area of science and technology, Dr. Kalam helped establish India as a nuclear power and oversaw the development of several indigenous missiles, including the Agni and Prithvi series. He also played a key role in increasing cooperation between India and Russia in the field of space research.
  • Achievement 2: Dr APJ Abdul Kalam’s Contribution to Missile Project : In 1998, Kalam and Dr Soma Raju collaborated to design a low-cost coronary stent. The stent was dubbed the “Kalam-Raju Stent.” For rural health care, the “Kalam-Raju Tablet” was created in 2012. The Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998 proved India’s nuclear capabilities. As the leader of India’s missile programme in the 1980s, political parties came together in 2002 to elect APJ Abdul Kalam as the country’s eleventh president.
  • Achievement 3: Missile Man of the country : In 1960, Dr Kalam joined the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) (DRDO) after he finished his graduation from the Madras Institute of Technology. In DRDO, Kalam was responsible for designing helicopters for the Indian Army. Dr Kalam was selected as the project director for SLV-III, India’s first indigenous satellite launch vehicle, after he joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO).

Legacy

  • Pioneering Space Exploration and Defense: Dr. Kalam's contributions to India's defense capabilities are arguably his most enduring legacy. He spearheaded the Integrated Guided Missile Development Program (IGMDP), propelling India's ballistic missile technology to new heights. As the founder of the Integrated Guided Missile Development Program (IGMDP), Kalam oversaw the development of a range of ballistic missiles, including Prithvi, Agni, and Akash, which enhanced India's defense capabilities.
  • Inspiring Generations: Dr. Kalam, often referred to as the "Missile Man of India," devoted his life to teaching and inspiring generations. He believed that education was the key to India's progress and actively engaged with students and youth. Dr. Kalam played a pivotal role in the development of India's cryogenic rocket engine technology, which was crucial for the country's heavy-lift launch vehicles and interplanetary missions.

  • Visionary Leader: Dr. Kalam was a visionary leader who believed that science and technology could be used to solve some of the world's most pressing problems, such as poverty, hunger, and disease. He advocated for sustainable development and inclusive growth.
